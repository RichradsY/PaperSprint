# ECIS 2026 Sprint 3 Construct Memo

Updated: 2026-03-10 23:40:00 CET

## Purpose

This memo sharpens the focal construct for the TREO paper and closes backlog item `B11`.

## Core Definition

For this paper, worker dignity is the worker's standing as a recognized, accountable, and non-humiliated professional in AI-mediated work. The construct is broader than simple task discretion because it concerns not only whether workers can act, but also how they are treated, how blame is allocated, and whether they retain legitimate voice when challenging AI-supported decisions. This follows the dignity literature's emphasis on recognition, respect, and non-degradation in work relations rather than only on internal motivation or job affect (Hodson, 2001; Lucas, 2015).

## Inclusion Logic

Use `worker dignity` when the argument concerns whether enterprise GenAI governance preserves meaningful professional judgment, intelligible accountability, protection from humiliating surveillance, and the ability to contest AI outputs without status loss. In this paper, dignity is the worker-level outcome through which governance regimes become socially and organizationally consequential.

## Exclusion Logic

Do not use `worker dignity` as a loose synonym for autonomy, well-being, fairness, or control. Those constructs overlap with dignity but do not exhaust it.

## Distinction From Adjacent Constructs

### Dignity vs. Autonomy

Autonomy concerns volition and self-direction in action (Gagne & Deci, 2005). Dignity is wider because a worker may retain some local discretion yet still suffer dignity loss if AI use exposes them to degrading monitoring, forced compliance, or individualized blame. In this paper, autonomy is one component of dignity, not the whole construct.

### Dignity vs. Well-Being

Well-being concerns affective, psychological, or health-related states. Dignity is a relational and normative condition about standing and treatment at work (Hodson, 2001; Lucas, 2015). A worker could report acceptable well-being while still perceiving dignity threats if organizational AI governance treats them as a replaceable executor rather than a trusted professional.

### Dignity vs. Control

Control is a property of the governance regime, not the focal outcome. Algorithmic control describes how rules, monitoring, and evaluation systems channel behavior (Kellogg et al., 2020). Dignity is what may be protected or undermined by those control arrangements.

## Operational Implication For The Paper

The paper should therefore present dignity as a composite worker-level outcome with four observable dimensions:

1. retained discretion over whether and how to use copilot outputs
2. intelligible accountability for final decisions and failures
3. protection from excessive or humiliating monitoring
4. preservation of professional voice when contesting AI suggestions

## Usage Rule

When drafting the paper, reserve the term `dignity` for arguments about worker standing, recognition, non-humiliation, and accountable professional agency. Use `autonomy`, `well-being`, and `control` only when those narrower constructs are specifically meant.
