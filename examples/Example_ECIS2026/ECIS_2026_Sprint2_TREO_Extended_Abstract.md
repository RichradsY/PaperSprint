## Title

Productive but Pressured? Governing Enterprise GenAI Copilots Without Eroding Worker Dignity

## Extended Abstract

### Motivation

Organizations are deploying enterprise generative AI copilots to accelerate knowledge work, improve consistency, and raise output quality. This trend is especially visible in professional service settings where consultants, analysts, and advisory staff now use AI assistance for client-facing writing, synthesis, and analytical tasks. Existing discussion often treats these systems as adoption or productivity tools, asking whether employees use them and whether performance improves. That framing is too narrow for an IS community increasingly concerned with the consequences of digital technologies for work, organizing, and society. In professional service work, AI assistance does not merely support task execution. It also redistributes discretion, visibility, and accountability.

This paper starts from a paradox. The same copilot governance choices that make enterprise GenAI attractive to firms may also make it problematic for workers. Greater visibility of prompts, outputs, and correction behavior can help organizations coordinate quality and scale expertise. At the same time, such visibility may intensify monitoring, narrow professional judgment, and expose workers to blame when AI-assisted outputs are wrong. Technologies marketed as empowering can therefore become infrastructures of subtle pressure when workers are expected to rely on AI, defend AI-supported outputs, and remain personally accountable for failure. The issue is not simply whether AI is ethical. The issue is how enterprise governance choices shape the conditions under which AI assistance is experienced as enablement or as algorithmic pressure.

### Research Question

How do enterprise GenAI copilot governance choices shape the balance between productivity and worker dignity in professional service knowledge work?

### Conceptual Framing

The paper proposes a governance-based explanation anchored in two complementary ideas: affordances and algorithmic control. An affordance perspective helps explain how GenAI copilots create perceived possibilities for faster drafting, broader information access, and more standardized work. An algorithmic-control perspective explains how deployment rules, monitoring arrangements, and accountability structures can simultaneously constrain how workers engage with those possibilities. Taken together, these lenses allow the paper to move beyond generic responsible-AI rhetoric and specify a mechanism through which organizational design choices shape worker experience.

The core argument is that worker dignity in AI-mediated work depends less on the mere presence of a copilot than on the governance regime around it. In this draft, worker dignity is defined as the worker's ability to perform AI-mediated tasks without being reduced to a replaceable executor, unfairly exposed to blame, or stripped of meaningful professional judgment. Four provisional dimensions matter: retained discretion over whether and how to use copilot outputs; intelligible accountability for final decisions; protection from humiliating or excessive monitoring; and preservation of professional voice when contesting AI suggestions.

The paper argues that four governance conditions are especially consequential: transparency, overrideability, monitoring intensity, and task criticality. Transparent systems can support reflection and learning, but transparency that primarily serves surveillance may undermine worker standing. Overrideability preserves professional judgment when AI outputs are weak, while low overrideability increases pressure to comply with machine suggestions. Monitoring intensity shapes whether AI use is framed as developmental support or behavioral control. Task criticality matters because the dignity stakes are higher when workers remain publicly accountable for high-visibility client-facing outputs. In combination, these conditions shape whether the copilot is experienced as an enabling partner or as an instrument of algorithmic pressure.

### Emerging Propositions

1. Enterprise GenAI copilots are more likely to support dignity-preserving productivity when workers retain meaningful overrideability over AI outputs.
2. Monitoring-intensive copilot regimes are more likely to convert perceived AI affordances into experiences of algorithmic pressure.
3. The negative dignity effects of high monitoring and low overrideability are stronger in high-criticality, client-facing professional service tasks.
4. Governance regimes that combine transparency with shared accountability are more likely to sustain both productivity and worker dignity than regimes that individualize blame for AI-assisted failure.

### Expected Contribution

The paper contributes to IS research in three ways. First, it reframes enterprise GenAI deployment as a governance problem rather than only an adoption or performance problem. Second, it gives conceptual precision to a human-centered outcome that is frequently invoked but rarely specified in operational IS terms. Third, it identifies boundary conditions under which productivity gains are compatible with responsible digital technology use in organizations. The broader significance is that the paper connects enterprise AI governance to enduring IS concerns about humane work design, accountability, and organizing under digital transformation.

### Empirical Roadmap

Although this TREO submission is conceptual, it is designed to seed a later empirical paper. The most plausible next step is a comparative qualitative case study of two to three professional service organizations, or business units within them, that have actively deployed GenAI copilots. Data could include semi-structured interviews, internal governance documents, training materials, and workflow artifacts. The comparison would focus on how different governance regimes shape worker experience under varying levels of task criticality and monitoring intensity. This roadmap matters because it shows that the conceptual argument can be translated into a credible empirical program rather than remaining a thought piece.
