## Working Title

Productive but Pressured? Governing Enterprise GenAI Copilots Without Eroding Worker Dignity

## Scope Statement

This paper examines how enterprise GenAI copilot deployments in professional service firms shape worker dignity when employees use AI assistance for client-facing analytical and writing tasks under organizational productivity pressure.

## Motivating Paradox

Enterprise GenAI copilots are being introduced into professional service work to increase speed, standardization, and output quality. Yet the same governance choices that make copilots attractive to firms can also make them problematic for workers. When prompts, outputs, and correction behavior become more visible, organizations gain new levers for coordination and quality control, but workers may also experience tighter surveillance, weaker professional judgment, and greater personal exposure when AI-assisted outputs fail. The paradox is that systems marketed as empowerment technologies can become infrastructures of subtle pressure if workers are expected to rely on them, defend them, and remain accountable for their errors at the same time. This creates an IS problem, not just an ethics problem: organizational design choices around transparency, overrideability, monitoring, and task criticality may determine whether the copilot is experienced as an enabling partner or as an instrument of algorithmic pressure.

## Research Question

How do enterprise GenAI copilot governance choices shape the balance between productivity and worker dignity in professional service knowledge work?

## Contribution Claim

The paper aims to shift enterprise AI research from a narrow adoption or performance framing toward a governance-based explanation of when GenAI copilots support productive knowledge work without degrading worker dignity.

## Theory Shortlist

### Preferred Theory Path

**Affordance perspective combined with algorithmic control**

- Why this path: it explains both the enabling side of copilots and the constraining side of governance mechanisms.
- Core logic: copilot features and deployment rules create a bundle of perceived action possibilities and constraints; monitoring and accountability arrangements determine whether these affordances are experienced as support or pressure.
- What it gives the paper: a mechanism-based explanation instead of a generic ethics argument.

### Secondary Candidate

**Socio-technical systems perspective**

- Why keep it in reserve: it is useful for framing the joint optimization problem between technical productivity and humane work design.
- Why it is not the lead path: by itself it risks staying too high-level and descriptive for reviewer scrutiny.

## Working Construct Definition

For this paper, worker dignity is the worker's ability to perform AI-mediated tasks without being reduced to a replaceable executor, unfairly exposed to blame, or stripped of meaningful professional judgment. In operational terms, the construct will be developed through four dimensions:

1. retained discretion over whether and how to use copilot outputs
2. intelligible accountability for errors and final decisions
3. protection from humiliating or excessive monitoring
4. preservation of professional voice when contesting AI suggestions

## Boundary Conditions

- The argument is strongest in high-visibility knowledge work where employees remain accountable for deliverable quality.
- The framework is less informative in low-discretion routine work where algorithmic management is already highly formalized.
- The argument assumes organizational rather than consumer AI use.
- The strongest dignity tensions should appear when firms simultaneously push AI adoption and maintain individual accountability for output failure.
